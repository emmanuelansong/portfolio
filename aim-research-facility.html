A.I.M. Research Facility Hero Shooter Level Design

A stylized domination map concept built in Unreal Engine 5, designed for a 5v5 hero shooter inspired by Marvel Rivals. The project explores how a single central objective, multi-route rotations, controlled verticality, and clear combat readability can support repeated team fights around one point. The level combines gameplay-first blockout thinking, route-framing, and environmental storytelling inside an A.I.M. sci-fi facility setting.

// quick overview

What this project is about

Summary

A.I.M. Research Facility – Sector 7 is a single-point domination map for a 5v5 hero shooter. The design centers on a single control point that creates repeated, high-intensity engagements around a readable central arena. The goal was to create a layout that feels competitive and fair while still supporting the spectacle, movement, and team synergy expected from modern hero shooters.

Project Goal

The main objective was to design a blockout concept that emphasizes strong central fights, balanced travel times, and multiple re-entry routes into the point. The level is structured around a readable central objective with flanks, controlled vertical access, and clear engagement zones that support both fast decision-making and long-term mastery.

My Role

I was responsible for the full level design pipeline, including research, reference gathering, 2D layout design, flow analysis, blockout development, and visual gameplay communication. I also explored interactive material ideas, destructible-space readability, and objective-space presentation in UE5.

// goals & restrictions

Design goals and constraints

Core goals

This map was designed to create centralized team fights around one objective, encourage rotations and re-engagements from multiple directions, and support structured combat in controlled spaces. The intended experience rewards positioning, timing, and team coordination rather than chaotic, unreadable brawls.

Layout constraints

To keep the map competitively fair and readable, I worked within a set of design constraints: a single central objective, near-symmetrical team access, at least three routes into the point, limited but meaningful verticality, and balanced travel times from each spawn. These restrictions helped keep the scope focused while grounding the design in hero-shooter fundamentals.

// research foundation

Research and design pillars

Research Marvel Rivals
Gameplay-first map philosophy

My research focused on how Marvel Rivals approaches level design through readability, multiple routes, hero expression, and environmental interactivity. One of the main takeaways was that maps should feel simple to understand at first glance while still enabling complex moment-to-moment decision-making through movement, ability usage, and changing combat spaces.

Flow Combat
Repeated engagements around one objective

Because this is a domination map, the level was designed around cyclical engagements: teams enter, contest, reposition, and re-enter the same objective space from different lanes. This required clear route-framing, balanced timing windows, and enough variation in approach paths to prevent the point from becoming a static choke.

Readability Interactivity
Spectacle versus clarity

A major design tension was balancing visual spectacle with gameplay clarity. My references and notes highlighted how hero shooters benefit from dynamic spaces and destruction, but only when those systems remain legible. That informed decisions around controlled verticality, selective environmental emphasis, and clear objective-space communication.

Main point

The central objective is intentionally exposed enough to create constant contest pressure, but it is framed by surrounding geometry and adjacent cover spaces so teams can stage pushes, collapse from multiple angles, and retake with coordinated timing.

Route structure

The layout uses a multi-route structure with direct approaches, side entries, and supporting flank paths. The goal was to ensure each team has meaningful decisions on entry, retreat, and re-engagement without overcomplicating the silhouette of the map.

Combat framing

Choke points and clash points were deliberately mapped to control where teams first collide, where pressure builds, and where positional advantage matters most. Health pack placement supports sustained skirmishes without undermining the importance of controlling the objective.

// design breakdown

Key level design decisions

Flow Routes
Three-direction pressure on point

One of the most important goals was ensuring the point could be contested from multiple directions without becoming visually noisy or tactically random. This helps domination rounds stay active and gives teams more than one viable way to break entrenched positions.

Readability Combat
Readable central arena

The center of the map is designed to feel contestable, open, and legible. It is not a pure open circle; instead, surrounding structures and adjacent pockets create moments of cover, line-of-sight denial, and tactical staging while preserving awareness of where the fight is happening.

Verticality Hero Design
Limited but meaningful verticality

Vertical spaces are used selectively to support hero-shooter movement and high-ground decisions without making the layout overwhelming. The aim was to create meaningful elevation choices rather than constant layered chaos.

Visual Design Feedback
Gameplay communication through materials

Alongside the level layout, I explored material prototypes for objective overlays, out-of-bounds barriers, and destructible surface highlighting. These were intended to support a faster hero-shooter readability standard where the environment communicates gameplay state clearly.

Process and iteration

Focus Areas

Route-framing, point-centric combat flow, readability, verticality, hero-movement support, and environment feedback systems.

Step 1
Competitive map research
I began by researching Marvel Rivals and other competitive shooters to understand how their maps structure rotation, objective pressure, and repeated engagements. I focused on how readable layouts can still support complex hero interactions, especially through route choice, combat ranges, and vertical access.
Step 2
Design document and layout goals
Before blockout, I defined a clear design brief: one central control point, at least three entry routes, near-symmetrical fairness, strong readability, and controlled verticality. This design document became the foundation for evaluating every layout decision and keeping the project scoped correctly.
Step 3
2D routing and flow analysis
I then translated the brief into 2D layouts, marking pathways, choke points, clash points, and health pack locations. This stage was useful for simplifying the map into its most important gameplay relationships before investing further in blockout and art direction.
Step 4
Environment theme and reference gathering
Once the gameplay layout was established, I gathered references around A.I.M. laboratories, Marvel sci-fi facilities, and clean, readable interior spaces. These references helped shape the facility’s identity while keeping the environment grounded in gameplay-first priorities.
Step 5
Blockout, materials, and gameplay communication
In Unreal Engine 5, I began translating the 2D plan into a playable space and explored supporting materials for objective readability, out-of-bounds barriers, and destructible-space communication. These experiments helped reinforce the core goal of making dynamic hero-shooter spaces readable under pressure.
